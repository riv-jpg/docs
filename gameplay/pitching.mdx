Pitching puts you in full command of the mound. Your goal is to retire batters by combining smart pitch selection, precise location, and deceptive sequencing — keeping hitters off balance and out of rhythm. Whether you're blowing high fastballs past free swingers or working curveballs in the dirt on two-strike counts, mastering the pitching system is what separates dominant aces from hittable arms.

## The Pitching Interface

From the mound, the camera locks into a catcher's-eye view. Three interface elements guide every pitch:

- **Zone Grid** — a nine-box representation of the strike zone; drag your crosshair to set your target location
- **Pitch Selector Wheel** — a swipe-based or button-mapped menu for choosing your pitch type
- **Power Meter** — a vertical bar that fills when you press the throw button; release in the green zone for optimal control

## Pitch Types

Your pitcher has access to six pitches, each with a unique speed, movement profile, and ideal usage situation.

<Accordion title="4-Seam Fastball — Pure Velocity">
  The fastest pitch in your arsenal and your primary weapon for setting up everything else. The 4-Seam Fastball travels with minimal horizontal or vertical movement, arriving on a straight, downhill plane.

  - **Best location:** Elevated in the zone — high fastballs generate more swings and misses and weak pop-ups
  - **Use when:** You need a strike, are ahead in the count, or want to blow a swing-and-miss past an off-balance hitter
  - **Watch out for:** Leaving it flat in the middle of the zone — a well-timed batter can do serious damage to a flat heater
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="2-Seam Fastball (Sinker) — Ground Ball Machine">
  Slightly slower than the 4-Seam but significantly more effective at inducing contact on the ground. The Sinker runs arm-side and drops late, making it difficult to elevate.

  - **Best location:** Low and in, or low and away depending on the batter's handedness
  - **Use when:** You need a ground ball — especially with runners on base and a double-play opportunity
  - **Watch out for:** Leaving it up in the zone; a sinker that doesn't sink becomes a batting practice fastball
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Curveball — Dramatic Downward Break">
  The Curveball is your biggest breaker. It drops sharply out of the zone after appearing to start at the belt — making it devastating when thrown for a strike early in the count or as a chase pitch below the zone.

  - **Best location:** Start it at the belt and break it into the lower third, or bounce it in the dirt for a strikeout chase
  - **Use when:** You're ahead in the count and want a swing-and-miss or a called third strike
  - **Watch out for:** Hanging curveballs — a curve that doesn't break lands belt-high in the heart of the zone and gets punished hard
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Slider — Sharp Lateral Break">
  The Slider cuts laterally toward your throwing-hand side with a tight, late break. It's harder for same-handed batters to recognize and reach than a curveball, making it your go-to strikeout pitch against batters who share your pitcher's handedness.

  - **Best location:** Glove-side edge of the zone or just off the outside corner
  - **Use when:** You're facing a same-handed batter who's sitting on your fastball
  - **Watch out for:** Leaving it over the middle to an opposite-handed batter — they can drive outside sliders with authority
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Changeup — The Great Deceiver">
  The Changeup is thrown with identical arm speed and release mechanics to your fastball, but arrives 8–12 mph slower. It disrupts a batter's timing completely — causing early swings, weak ground balls, and harmless pop-ups.

  - **Best location:** Low and away, finishing off the outside part of the plate
  - **Use when:** You've established your fastball and the batter is clearly sitting on it; the timing disruption is most effective after 2–3 fastballs
  - **Watch out for:** Throwing it early in counts before you've set up the fastball — a batter who hasn't seen your heater yet won't be fooled by the speed difference
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Cutter — Late-Breaking Fastball">
  The Cut Fastball looks like a regular fastball until the last few feet, when it darts slightly toward the glove side. It's faster than a slider but with just enough movement to jam opposite-handed hitters or nick the inside corner.

  - **Best location:** Inside to opposite-hand batters; at the knees on the inner half
  - **Use when:** You need to move a crowding batter's hands or break a bat — the late cut is nearly impossible to fully extend on
  - **Watch out for:** Cutting it too far into the zone; an over-cut cutter loses velocity and can end up flat
</Accordion>

## Selecting and Throwing a Pitch

<Steps>
  <Step title="Select Your Pitch Type">
    Open the pitch selector wheel by swiping on the wheel icon (mobile) or pressing the assigned shortcut key (1–6 on keyboard; left stick flick on gamepad). Highlight the pitch you want to throw.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Set Your Target Location">
    Drag the crosshair across the zone grid to your intended target. You can aim inside, outside, up, or down — or off the edges of the zone for waste pitches. Think about the batter's known weaknesses before committing.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Initiate the Pitch">
    Press the throw button to start the pitch. The power meter begins filling immediately. Watch it climb toward the green zone.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Release at the Green Zone">
    Release the throw button when the meter reaches the green zone for maximum accuracy. Releasing too early or too late reduces precision and increases the gap between your intended target and the actual pitch location.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Watch the Result">
    The ball travels toward the plate. Your **Control stat** determines how tightly the actual location clusters around your target — high Control means the ball lands close to your crosshair; low Control introduces a wider scatter radius.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Pitch Sequencing Strategy

Throwing hard isn't enough — elite pitchers win by thinking one pitch ahead. Follow these principles to keep batters guessing:

- **Establish your fastball first.** Get the batter thinking about velocity before introducing off-speed pitches. A changeup is worthless if the hitter hasn't seen your fastball yet.
- **Work ahead in the count.** Throwing a first-pitch strike puts the batter on defense and gives you the freedom to throw more breaking balls and waste pitches.
- **Mix locations, not just pitch types.** A fastball inside followed by a fastball away is more disruptive than two pitches in the same spot.
- **Don't repeat yourself.** Throwing the same pitch to the same location three times in a row lets the batter time and track it. Batters adapt — always be one step ahead.
- **Use breaking balls when ahead, fastballs when behind.** When you're 0-2, attack with your best strikeout pitch. When you're 3-1, throw a strike — give the batter something to hit but make it on your terms.

<Tip>
  After a swing-and-miss on a high fastball, drop the very next pitch low in the zone — a curveball or sinker works perfectly. Batters who just missed a high heater often chase low breaking balls expecting another fastball.
</Tip>

## Stamina and Fatigue

Every pitch your pitcher throws draws down on their **Stamina stat**. As Stamina depletes, your pitcher's performance degrades in two ways: velocity drops and your Control scatter radius widens.

- **Above 60% Stamina** — Full velocity and normal Control accuracy
- **30–60% Stamina** — Minor velocity drop; slight Control degradation on off-speed pitches
- **Below 30% Stamina** — A fatigue indicator flashes on the HUD; noticeable velocity loss; Control becomes inconsistent; wild pitch probability increases

<Warning>
  When the fatigue indicator appears, avoid throwing low-Control pitches like the curveball or changeup near the edges of the zone. Off-target breaking balls in fastball counts are exactly what power hitters are looking for.
</Warning>

In **Story Mode**, you can pull yourself from the game when Stamina runs low and hand the ball to an AI closer to protect the lead. In **Quick Play**, you pitch the full game — manage your pitch count carefully and finish batters off quickly to preserve energy for late innings.

## The Strikeout System

Strikeouts are the cleanest way to retire a batter — no fielding required. To record a strikeout, you need three strikes:

- **Swinging strike** — the batter swings and misses, or tips a foul with two strikes already recorded
- **Called strike** — the batter takes a pitch that lands in the strike zone without swinging

<Note>
  Foul balls always count as strikes unless the batter already has two strikes. Use this to your advantage — getting a batter to foul off your fastball runs up the count and sets up your strikeout pitch.
</Note>

**Wild pitches** are a risk when your Stamina is low or your Control stat is underdeveloped. A wild pitch that gets past the catcher lets any baserunner advance — potentially scoring a run or putting a runner in scoring position. Keep your pitch location disciplined, especially with runners on base.
